🚨 **Stop — there's a critical problem with the opener vs. non-opener comparison before we go any further.**

**Opening the email is caused by the email.** That means "openers" and "non-openers" aren't comparable groups — they're already self-selected by their response to treatment. People who open discount emails tend to be more price-sensitive, more engaged, and more likely to purchase *regardless* of the email. Comparing them to non-openers doesn't measure the email's effect — it mostly measures *who opens emails*.

This is the **post-treatment conditioning trap**: you're filtering your population on a variable that was affected by the treatment itself, which introduces selection bias and will almost certainly overestimate the email's effect.

---

**The valid comparison is:**

> All 100K who received the email **vs.** a control group who did *not* receive it

Which brings me to my first real question:

**Was this email sent to all 100,000 customers, or did some customers not receive it?**

- If some customers were held out (randomly or otherwise) → we have a control group to work with, and this could be a clean experiment or quasi-experiment.
- If everyone got the email → there's no natural control group, and we have a harder identification problem.

Which is it?
